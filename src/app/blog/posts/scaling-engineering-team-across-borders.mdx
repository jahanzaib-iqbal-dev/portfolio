## It Started With Two People

When I co-founded Global Software Consulting, the "team" was me and one other developer. Today we're 40+ engineers. The hardest part wasn't hiring — it was building systems that keep everyone productive, growing, and shipping quality work at scale.

Here's what actually works.

## The Three Pillars of a Productive Engineering Team

### 1. Ownership Over Assignment

The single biggest shift that transformed our productivity was moving from task assignment to ownership.

**What this looks like:**
- Engineers own features end-to-end — from understanding the requirement to shipping it
- Nobody waits to be told what to do next. The backlog is transparent, priorities are clear
- Every PR has a "why" — not just what changed, but why it matters

**What we stopped doing:**
- Micromanaging daily tasks
- Assigning work in standup meetings
- Treating engineers as "resources" instead of owners

When people own their work, they care about quality differently. They think about edge cases because it's *their* feature, not just a ticket.

### 2. Systems That Scale Without Me

At 5 engineers, I could review every PR and be in every decision. At 40+, that's impossible. So I built systems:

**Code Quality:**
- Every PR gets reviewed. No exceptions. Not even mine
- CI runs on every push — tests, linting, type checking
- Automated formatting. Zero style debates in PRs
- Architecture Decision Records for significant technical choices

**Communication:**
- Async by default. We don't schedule meetings to share information — we write it down
- PRs have detailed descriptions. Decisions are documented in tickets
- Weekly standups are 15 minutes, focused on blockers — not status updates
- Documentation is treated as a first-class deliverable, not an afterthought

**Onboarding:**
- New engineers ship their first PR in the first week
- Every project has a README that actually explains how to run it
- Pair programming for the first two weeks, then gradually independent

### 3. Growth as a Retention Strategy

The engineers who stay longest aren't the ones with the highest salary — they're the ones who are growing.

**How we invest in growth:**
- Senior engineers mentor juniors through code review, not lectures
- Engineers rotate across projects to broaden their skills
- We allocate time for open-source contributions and learning
- Technical decisions are made collaboratively, not top-down

**What we measure:**
- PR quality and review thoroughness (not just velocity)
- Knowledge sharing — are you making the team better?
- Independence — can you own a feature without hand-holding?

## Hiring: What I Look For

After hiring 40+ engineers, here's what actually predicts success:

### Matters Most
1. **Ownership mindset** — Do they propose solutions or wait for instructions?
2. **Written communication** — Can they explain their thinking clearly in a PR description?
3. **Self-direction** — Can they unblock themselves or do they wait?
4. **Curiosity** — Do they ask "why" or just implement what's asked?

### Matters Less Than You Think
- Years of experience (some of our best engineers are 2-3 years in)
- Specific technology expertise (good engineers learn fast)
- Prestigious degrees or company names

### Red Flags
- "I just do what I'm told" mentality
- Can't explain the reasoning behind their own code
- No side projects, no curiosity, no opinions about software

## The Daily Operating System

Here's how 40+ engineers stay aligned without constant meetings:

**Monday:** Team leads sync (30 min) — what's shipping this week, any blockers
**Daily:** Async standups in Slack — what I did, what I'm doing, any blockers
**Thursday:** Optional tech talk (30 min) — someone shares something they learned
**Friday:** Ship day — we try to merge and deploy by end of day

**What we DON'T do:**
- Daily video standups with 40 people
- Long planning meetings every sprint
- Status update emails

## Mistakes I Made Scaling from 5 to 40

### Mistake 1: Trying to Be in Every Decision
At 10 engineers, I was still reviewing every PR and approving every architecture decision. I became the bottleneck. **Fix:** Built a tech lead layer. Gave them decision-making authority. My job shifted from doing to enabling.

### Mistake 2: Hiring for Speed Instead of Culture
When we had urgent client deadlines, I hired fast without enough culture screening. Some technically great engineers were terrible collaborators. **Fix:** Added a "working session" to the interview — pair on a real problem for 30 minutes. Reveals collaboration style instantly.

### Mistake 3: Not Investing in Documentation Early
At 5 people, tribal knowledge works. At 20, it's chaos. We lost weeks when key engineers went on vacation and nobody knew how their systems worked. **Fix:** Documentation became a deliverable. "If it's not documented, it's not done."

### Mistake 4: One-Size-Fits-All Management
Junior engineers need structure and guidance. Senior engineers need autonomy and challenge. I was managing everyone the same way. **Fix:** Tailored management — more check-ins for juniors, more freedom for seniors.

## What I'd Tell a Founder Building Their First Team

1. **Hire slow, fire fast.** One toxic hire damages the team more than being understaffed
2. **Build systems before you need them.** CI/CD, code review practices, documentation — set these up at 5 engineers, not 50
3. **Your job changes.** At 5 engineers, you code. At 15, you review. At 40, you build systems and develop leaders. Let go of being the best coder in the room
4. **Culture is what you tolerate.** If you tolerate bad PRs, bad communication, or missed deadlines — that becomes your culture
5. **Invest in your tech leads.** They are the multiplier. One great tech lead makes 10 engineers better

## The Results

- **40+ engineers** and growing
- **155+ projects** delivered successfully

The biggest lesson: scaling a team isn't about hiring more people. It's about building systems that make every person more effective than they'd be anywhere else.
