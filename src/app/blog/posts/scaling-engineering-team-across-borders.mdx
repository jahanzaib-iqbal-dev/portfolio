## The Challenge

When I co-founded Global Software Consulting in 2016, it was just a few developers in Lahore. Today, we're 40+ engineers across five countries. Scaling a distributed team is one of the hardest things I've done — and one of the most rewarding.

Here's what actually works.

## Communication Is Architecture

In a distributed team, your communication structure IS your architecture. Conway's Law isn't just a theory — it's reality.

### What We Do

- **Async by default.** We don't schedule meetings to share information. We write it down. PRs have detailed descriptions. Decisions are documented in tickets.
- **Sync for alignment.** Weekly standups are short (15 min) and focused on blockers, not status updates. We use them to stay aligned, not to report.
- **Overlap hours.** Every team has at least 4 hours of overlap with their collaborators. We hire with this in mind.

## Hiring Across Borders

### What I Look For

1. **Written communication skills.** In a distributed team, if you can't write clearly, you can't collaborate effectively. This is non-negotiable.
2. **Self-direction.** Remote engineers need to unblock themselves. Waiting for someone in another timezone to answer a question is expensive.
3. **Ownership mindset.** The best distributed engineers don't ask "what should I do?" — they propose solutions and ask for feedback.

### What Doesn't Matter

- **Same timezone.** We have engineers in PKT, CET, EST, GMT, and AEST. It works because of async practices, not because of clock alignment.
- **Years of experience.** Some of our best engineers are 2-3 years in. Hunger and ownership beat experience every time.

## Code Quality at Scale

With 40+ engineers, code quality doesn't maintain itself. Here's our system:

### Every PR Gets Reviewed

No exceptions. Not even mine. This is the single most important practice for maintaining quality at scale.

### Automated Everything

- **CI runs on every push.** Tests, linting, type checking.
- **Deployment is automated.** No manual deploys, ever.
- **Code formatting is automated.** No style debates in PRs.

### Architecture Decision Records

When we make significant technical decisions, we write them down. Why we chose this database, why we structured the API this way, why we picked this library. Future engineers (and future us) need context, not just code.

## The Hard Parts

### Time Zones Are Real

No matter how good your async practices are, sometimes you need to talk in real-time. When your team spans 13 hours of timezone difference, someone is always compromising. We rotate meeting times so the burden is shared.

### Culture Doesn't Build Itself

In an office, culture happens in hallways and lunch rooms. Remote, you have to be intentional. We do:
- Monthly virtual team hangouts (no work talk)
- Annual in-person meetups
- Public recognition in team channels

### Trust Takes Longer

Building trust remotely takes more time and more effort. Over-communicate early. Share context generously. Give people the benefit of the doubt.

## The Results

- **40+ engineers** across Pakistan, Netherlands, Canada, UK, and Australia
- **155+ projects** delivered successfully
- **Multi-year client relationships** — our average engagement is 2+ years
- **Low turnover** — engineers stay because they own their work

## What I'd Tell Someone Starting Today

Start with async practices from day one, even if your team is in the same room. The habits you build when you're small are the habits that scale. And hire for writing skills — it's the most underrated engineering skill in distributed teams.
